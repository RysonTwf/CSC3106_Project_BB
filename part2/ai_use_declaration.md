# Appendix: AI-Use Declaration (Part 2)

Same tool and same general approach as Part 1, see `part1/ai_use_declaration.md` if you're reading these separately. In the final report the two get merged into a single appendix.

**Tool used:** Claude (Anthropic), through the web chat at claude.ai.

**What we used it for:** Drafting `detector.py`, `simulate_lockout.py`, and `authlog_parsing.py` (which is really just the Part 1 parsing logic carried over, so Part 1 and Part 2 can't quietly disagree about what counts as a failed attempt). It also drafted the fail2ban jail, the sshd hardening config, the rsyslog forwarder config, and the comments in each. The "prevent / detect / preserve" three-layer framing for the whole response was suggested too. We liked the structure and kept it, but we decided what actually went in each layer ourselves, based on what Part 1 found.

**How we checked it:** Same rule as Part 1: no number in the Part 2 section came from just asking the model. We ran `detector.py` and `simulate_lockout.py` against our own extract, and everything in the report traces back to `alerts.csv` or `lockout_simulation.csv` in `part2/output/`. The thresholds in `detector.py` (10 failures for R1, 25 for R2, 15 for R3) weren't numbers it suggested that we just accepted, we went back and measured what legitimate traffic in our own extract actually looks like first (worst legitimate burst is 9, worst legitimate hourly hit on a privileged account is 20), and picked thresholds that sit clearly above that. We also specifically checked that R1 reproduces the exact Part 1 finding (117 failures, then `deploy` compromised at 23:47:04) rather than assuming the logic was equivalent just because it looked similar.

Worth being upfront about one thing: `PermitRootLogin no` in the sshd config was suggested as generic "standard hardening" advice before we'd checked anything against our data. We only kept it because we went back and confirmed there isn't a single legitimate root SSH login anywhere in our extract, so disabling it doesn't break anything we can actually see. If that hadn't been true we'd have left it out, or at least flagged it as riskier than it looks.

**How much of this is AI vs. us:** First drafts of all the Part 2 code and configs were AI-generated. What we did ourselves: choosing Risk 3 over the other four risks as the one to build on (and writing out why, in the report), tuning every threshold against our own measured data instead of accepting suggested defaults, actually running the lockout simulation instead of just asserting the policy would work, and writing the limitations section ourselves, in particular the point that a real attacker would adapt, so the "67% blocked" figure describes the traffic we observed, not a guarantee about a future attack.

**How we made sure the final claims are Python-verified:** Every number in the Part 2 section can be found in `part2/output/alerts.csv` or `part2/output/lockout_simulation.csv`, or reproduced by rerunning `detector.py` and `simulate_lockout.py` with their default settings against `../1_auth.log.txt`.
